Finite element formulations

In this appendix we derive the finite element formulations of \(\eqref{eq:FiniteDifferenceSoluteTransport}\)-\(\eqref{eq:FiniteDifferenceDarcy}\) and \(\eqref{eq:FiniteDifferenceStreamfunctionPoisson}\)-\(\eqref{eq:FiniteDifferenceStreamfunctionVelocity}\).

In addition to being applicable in 3D and being capable of handling boundary conditions of prescribed pressure and non-zero prescribed, another advantage this method has over the 2D streamfunction formulation is that it does not require incompressibility. Retaining the \(\varepsilon\) term in [eqref], we have

\(\eqref{eq:FiniteDifferenceContinuity}\) and \(\eqref{eq:FiniteDifferenceDarcy}\)

The streamfunction equation \(\eqref{eq:FiniteDifferenceStreamfunctionPoisson}\) is readily identified as a tensor-weighted Poisson equation, and as such is very amenable to the finite element method. We derive the variational problem, that is to find \(\psi^n\in V_\psi\) such that

\[\begin{equation} \label{eq:VariationalStreamfunction} \int_{\partial\Omega}\text{d}\Gamma~v\,\textbf{n}\cdot\nabla\psi^n-\int_\Omega\text{d}\Omega~\nabla v\cdot\bigg(\frac{\mu^n\,(\mathsf{K}^n)^{\mathsf{T}}\cdot\nabla\psi^n}{\det(\mathsf{K}^n)}\bigg)=\int_\Omega\text{d}\Omega~v\cos\beta\frac{\partial\rho^n}{\partial x} - v\sin\beta\frac{\partial\rho^n}{\partial y} ~~~\forall v\in\hat{V}_\psi \end{equation}\]

by multiplying \(\eqref{eq:FiniteDifferenceStreamfunctionPoisson}\) by some test function \(v\), integrating over the domain \(\Omega\) and integrating the left-hand side by parts. \(V_\psi\) and \(\hat{V}_\psi\) are a suitably-chosen function spaces for the trial function \(\psi\) and the test function \(v\) respectively. We choose for the (discretized) trial and test function space

\[\begin{equation} V_\psi = \hat{V}_\psi = \{v\in H^1(\Omega)~,~v|_{\partial\Omega}=0~,~v|_{\mathcal{K}}\in\text{P}_k(\mathcal{K})~\forall\mathcal{K}\in\cup_{\mathcal{K}}\mathcal{K}\} \end{equation}\]

with \(k\geq2\), where \(\text{P}_k(\mathcal{K})\) denotes a Lagrange polynomial of degree \(k\) on the cell \(\mathcal{K}\) of the mesh \(\Omega\) and \(H^1(\Omega)\) denotes the Sobolev space. Since the test function vanishes on the boundary and the streamfunction boundary condition \(\psi|_{\partial\Omega}=0\) is a pure Dirichlet boundary condition, the above term for Neumann boundary conditions vanishes leaving

\[\begin{equation} F_\psi(\psi^n, v)\equiv -\int_\Omega\text{d}\Omega~\nabla v\cdot\bigg(\frac{\mu^n\,(\mathsf{K}^n)^{\mathsf{T}}\cdot\nabla\psi^n}{\det(\mathsf{K}^n)}\bigg) - v\cos\beta\frac{\partial\rho^n}{\partial x} + v\sin\beta\frac{\partial\rho^n}{\partial y}=0 ~~~\forall v\in\hat{V}_\psi \end{equation}\]

Whilst the advection expression \(\textbf{u}\cdot\nabla c\) in the transport equations \(\eqref{eq:FiniteDifferenceSoluteTransport}\) and \(\eqref{eq:FiniteDifferenceHeatTransport}\) could simply be written in terms of the streamfunction as \((\partial\psi/\partial y, -\partial\psi/\partial x)\cdot\nabla c\) to avoid having to directly compute the velocity prior to solving \(\eqref{eq:FiniteDifferenceSoluteTransport}\), in practice it is desirable to compute \(\textbf{u}\) throughout the course of the simulation in order to verify that no-flux boundary conditions are respected. Furthermore having \(\textbf{u}^n\) available simplifies the computation of secondary quantities such as the advective flux \(\eqref{eq:SolutalAdvectiveFlux}\), maximum speed \(\max_{\textbf{x}}|\textbf{u}^n|\) and CFL timestep \(\eqref{eq:TimestepCFL}\). For a streamfunction in the continuous Lagrange function space of degree \(k~(=2)\), it is appropritate to choose for velocity a vector-valued discontinuous Lagrange function space of degree \(k-1~(=1)\).

\[\begin{equation} V_{\textbf{u}} = \{\textbf{v}\in L^2(\Omega)\times L^2(\Omega)~,~\textbf{n}\cdot\textbf{v}|_{\partial\Omega}=0~,~\textbf{v}|_{\mathcal{K}}\in\text{DP}_{k-1}(\mathcal{K})\times\text{DP}_{k-1}(\mathcal{K})~\forall\mathcal{K}\in\cup_{\mathcal{K}}\mathcal{K}\} \end{equation}\]

incorporating the no-flux boundary conditions, where \(\text{DP}_k(\mathcal{K})\) denotes a discontinuous Lagrange polynomial of degree \(k\) on the cell \(\mathcal{K}\) of the mesh \(\Omega\) and \(L^2(\Omega)\) denotes the Lebesgue space. Solving for \(\textbf{u}\) can either be treated as the projection problem of finding \(\textbf{u}\in V_{\textbf{u}}\) such that

\[\begin{equation} F_{\textbf{u}}(\textbf{u}^n, \textbf{v})= \int_\Omega\text{d}\Omega~\textbf{v}\cdot\textbf{u}^n-\int_\Omega\text{d}\Omega~\textbf{v}\cdot\begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial\psi^n}{\partial y} \\ -\frac{\partial\psi^n}{\partial x} \end{pmatrix}~~~\forall\textbf{v}\in\hat{V}_{\textbf{u}} \end{equation}\]

or alternatively as the interpolation problem of the vector-valued expression \((\partial\psi^n/\partial y, -\partial\psi^n/\partial x)\) into the function space \(V_{\textbf{u}}\). The interpolation problem has the advantage of being less computationally demanding since no matrix assembly and inversion is required.

Firstly it is convenient to divide the transport equation \(\eqref{eq:FiniteDifferenceSoluteTransport}\) through by the porosity \(\phi\) so that the time derivative term is not multiplied by a non-constant coefficient. Then we choose for the (discretized) test and trial function spaces

\[\begin{align} V_c &= \{v\in H^1(\Omega)~,~v|_{\mathcal{K}}\in\text{P}_k(\mathcal{K})~\forall\mathcal{K}\in\cup_{\mathcal{K}}\mathcal{K}\} \\ \hat{V}_c &= \{v\in H^1(\Omega)~,~v|_{\partial\Omega}=0~,~v|_{\mathcal{K}}\in\text{P}_k(\mathcal{K})~\forall\mathcal{K}\in\cup_{\mathcal{K}}\mathcal{K}\} \end{align}\]

with degree \(k=1\) such that at the \(n^{\text{th}}\) integration step the variational problem is to find \(c^{n+1}\in V_c\) such that

\[\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \label{eq:TransportCGAppendix} F_c(c^{n+1}, v)&\equiv \int_\Omega\text{d}\Omega~v\frac{c^{n+1}-c^n}{\Delta t^n} \\ &\quad\quad +v\frac{\mathcal{D}_A(\textbf{u}\cdot\nabla c)}{\mathcal{D}_\phi(\phi)} \\ &\quad\quad +\frac{1}{Ra}\nabla\bigg(\frac{v}{\mathcal{D}_\phi(\phi)}\bigg)\cdot(\mathcal{D}_D(\mathsf{D}\cdot\nabla c)) \\ &\quad\quad + v\,Da\frac{\mathcal{D}_R(R)}{\mathcal{D}_\phi(\phi)} \\ &\quad -\int_{\partial\Omega}\text{d}\Omega~\frac{1}{Ra}v\textbf{n}\cdot(\mathsf{D}\cdot\nabla c) \\ &=0\qquad \forall v\in\hat{V}_c \end{aligned} \end{equation}\]

The boundary term vanishes by virtue of the no-flux boundary condition \(\textbf{n}\cdot(\mathsf{D}\cdot\nabla c)=0|_{\partial\Omega}\), which in the case of an isotropic dispersion \(\mathsf{D}\propto\mathsf{I}\) simplifies to \(\textbf{n}\cdot\nabla c=0|_{\partial\Omega}\). The finite difference operators \(\mathcal{D}_{\phi, A, D, R}\) act on the porosity, advection, diffusion and reaction expressions respectively. Since \(\textbf{u}^{n+1}\), \(\phi^{n+1}\) and \(s^{n+1}\) are not solved for prior to solving for \(c^{n+1}\) in the numerical integration algorithm \(\ref{alg:NumericalIntegration}\), the finite difference discretizations produced by \(\mathcal{D}_{\phi, A, D, R}\) must be explicit with respect to \(\textbf{u}\), \(\phi\) and \(s\).

The continuous Galerkin formulation of the advection-dominated transport equation (i.e. \(\eqref{eq:TransportCGAppendix}\) in the limit of large \(Ra\)) requires additional stabilization terms to prevent spurious oscillations in the numerical solution. The streamline-upwind Petrov-Galerkin (SUPG) [cite] method adds to \(\eqref{eq:TransportCGAppendix}\) the term

\[F_{\text{SUPG}}(c^{n+1}, v)=\int_\Omega\text{d}\Omega~\tau^n(\textbf{u}^n_{\text{eff}}\cdot\nabla )\mathcal{R}^{n+1}\]

where \(\tau^n\) is a stabilization parameter, \(\textbf{u}^n_{\text{eff}}\) is an effective velocity and

\[\begin{align} \label{eq:ResidualSUPG} \mathcal{R}^{n+1}&=\frac{c^{n+1}-c^n}{\Delta t^n} + \frac{\mathcal{D}_A(\textbf{u}\cdot\nabla c)}{\mathcal{D}_\phi(\phi)} - \frac{1}{Ra}\frac{\mathcal{D}_D(\nabla\cdot(\mathsf{D}\cdot\nabla c))}{\mathcal{D}_\phi(\phi)} - Da\frac{\mathcal{D}_R(R)}{\mathcal{D}_\phi(\phi)} \end{align}\]

is the residual. The choice of \(\tau^n\) optimal in successfully quelling spurious oscillations without introducing excessive numerical dissipation is an active topic of research, and as such there exist a number of possible expressions for \(\tau^n\) in terms of an effective velocity \(\textbf{u}^n_{\text{eff}}\), effective diffusivity \(\mathsf{D}^n_{\text{eff}}\) and reaction \(R^n_{\text{eff}}\), which are defined with reference to [eqref] cast in the form of the steady-state transport equation

\[\begin{align} \textbf{u}^n_{\text{eff}}\cdot\nabla c^{n+1}-\nabla\cdot(\mathsf{D}^n_{\text{eff}}\cdot\nabla c^{n+1})+R^n_{\text{eff}}c^{n+1}=S^n \end{align}\]

with source term \(S^n\) explicit in \(c\). In general \(\tau^n=\tau^n(\textbf{u}^n_{\text{eff}}, \mathsf{D}^n_{\text{eff}}, R^n_{\text{eff}}, \ell)\) given some local cell size measure \(\ell(\textbf{x})\). In the case of the 1D advection-diffusion equation with no reaction term, the optimal stabilization parameter is known to be

\[\begin{align} \tau^n = \frac{\ell}{2|\textbf{u}^n_{\text{eff}}|} \bigg(\text{coth}\bigg(\frac{|\textbf{u}^n_{\text{eff}}|\ell}{2D^n_{\text{eff}}}\bigg) - \frac{2D^n_{\text{eff}}}{|\textbf{u}^n_{\text{eff}}|\ell}\bigg) \end{align}\]

but, strictly speaking, this result does not generalize to higher dimensions. Nonetheless it can be used anyway in 2D or 3D with some success [cite]. [ref] suggests the form,

\[\begin{align} \label{eq:TauCodina} \tau^n &=\bigg(\frac{2|\textbf{u}^n_{\text{eff}}|}{\ell}+\frac{4D^n_{\text{eff}}}{\ell^2}+R^n_{\text{eff}}\bigg)^{-1} \end{align}\]

or alternatively following [ref]

\[\begin{align} \label{eq:TauShakib} \tau^n &=\bigg(\bigg(\frac{2|\textbf{u}^n_{\text{eff}}|}{\ell}\bigg)^2+9\bigg(\frac{4D^n_{\text{eff}}}{\ell^2}\bigg)^2+(R^n_{\text{eff}})^2\bigg)^{-1/2} \end{align}\]

In the discontinuous Galerkin (DG) method, we instead choose for the trial and test spaces

\[\begin{align} V_c = \hat{V}_c = \{v\in H^1(\Omega)~,~v|_{\mathcal{K}}\in\text{DP}_k(\mathcal{K})~\forall\mathcal{K}\in\cup_{\mathcal{K}}\mathcal{K}\} \end{align}\]

with degree \(k=1\). In contrast to the CG method, requirement of continuity at each mesh cell vertex is relaxed in favour of imposing local flux conservation across cell facets [rethink]. Dirichlet boundary conditions are also weakly imposed in the variational formulation, as opposed to being stronly imposed on the linear algebra. We denote by \(\{\cdot\}\) and \(\llbracket\cdot\rrbracket\) the cell facet average and jump operators defined by

\[\begin{align} \{v\}&=\frac{1}{2}(v^++v^-) \\ \{\textbf{v}\}&=\frac{1}{2}(\textbf{v}^++\textbf{v}^-) \\ \llbracket v \rrbracket &= v^+ - v^- \\ \llbracket\textbf{v}\rrbracket &= \textbf{n}^+\cdot\textbf{v}^+ - \textbf{n}^-\cdot\textbf{v}^- = \textbf{n}\cdot(\textbf{v}^+ - \textbf{v}^-) \end{align}\]

when acting on either a scalar \(v\) or vector \(\textbf{v}\). Here \(\textbf{n} = \textbf{n}^+ = -\textbf{n}^-\) is the outward unit normal on a cell facet. Following the lengthy derivation of [cite], the variational problem is to find \(c^{n+1}\in V_c\) such that

\[\begin{equation} \begin{aligned} \label{eq:AppendixTransportDG} F_c(c^{n+1},v)&\equiv \int_\Omega\text{d}\Omega~v\frac{c^{n+1}-c^n}{\Delta t^n} \\ &\quad\quad + ... \\ &\quad\quad + v\,Da\frac{\mathcal{D}_R(R)}{\mathcal{D}_\phi(\phi)} \\ &\quad -\int_{\partial\Omega}\text{d}\Omega~\frac{1}{Ra}v\textbf{n}\cdot(\mathsf{D}\cdot\nabla c) \\ &=0 \forall v\in\hat{V}_c \end{aligned} \end{equation}\]

Again in contrast to the CG formulation, Dirichlet boundary conditions are weakly imposed in the variational formulation by the penalty parameter \(\gamma_{\text{DG}}\) rather than being imposed strongly on the linear algebra.

Finite element formulations of the thermal transport equation \(\eqref{eq:FiniteDifferenceHeatTransport}\) are identical to those of the solute transport equation \(\eqref{eq:FiniteDifferenceSoluteTransport}\), except with terms relating the reaction omitted.

Since the evolution equation \(\eqref{eq:FiniteDifferenceEvolution}\) contains no spatial derivatives, it is essentially just a projection problem to be solved for \(s^{n+1}\).

\[\begin{equation} \label{eq:AppendixEvolution} F_s(s^{n+1},v) \equiv \int_\Omega\text{d}\Omega~v\frac{s^{n+1}-s^n}{\Delta t^n} + v\,\varepsilon Da\frac{\mathcal{D}_R(R)}{\varphi} =0\qquad \forall v\in\hat{V}_s \end{equation}\]

with function spaces \(V_s = \hat{V}_s = \{v\in H^1(\Omega)~,~v|_{\mathcal{K}}\in\text{P}_k(\mathcal{K})~\forall\mathcal{K}\in\cup_{\mathcal{K}}\mathcal{K}\}\). Alternatively, if \(\mathcal{D}_R\) is explicit with respect to \(s\) then \(\eqref{eq:FiniteDifferenceEvolution}\) may be rearranged explicitly for \(s^{n+1}\) and we have the interpolation problem of

\[s^{n+1} = s^{n} -\Delta t\,\varepsilon Da\frac{\mathcal{D}_R(R)}{\varphi}\]

into the function space \(V_s\). \(\mathcal{D}_R\) need not be explicit with respect to \(c\) since \(c^{n+1}\) is solved for before \(s^{n+1}\) in Algorithm \(\ref{alg:NumericalIntegration}\).
